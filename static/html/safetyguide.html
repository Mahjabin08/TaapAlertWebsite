Heatwaves can be dangerous, and prolonged exposure to extreme heat can
lead to serious health complications such as heat exhaustion, heatstroke,
dehydration, and more. To protect yourself and others, it's crucial to take
precautions and stay prepared. Below are important guidelines to help you
manage and stay safe during a heatwave.

During a heatwave, it’s important to stay indoors as much as possible,
especially during peak hours of heat, typically between 10 a.m. and 4 p.m.
If you have to go outside, try to limit strenuous activities, take frequent
breaks in the shade, and wear light, breathable clothing. Dark, heavy
materials trap heat, making you more vulnerable to heat-related illnesses.
Light-colored, loose-fitting fabrics allow better air circulation and help keep
your body cool.

Staying hydrated is key during hot weather. Drink plenty of water even if you
don’t feel thirsty, as heat can quickly dehydrate you without warning. Avoid
drinks that cause dehydration, such as alcohol and caffeinated beverages.
If you’re sweating heavily, it’s a good idea to replenish electrolytes by
drinking sports drinks or adding a pinch of salt to your water.

Create a cool environment indoors by keeping windows closed and curtains
drawn to block out sunlight. If possible, use fans or air conditioning to maintain
a comfortable temperature. If you don’t have air conditioning, consider visiting
public places such as cooling centers, libraries, or shopping malls during the
hottest part of the day. In the absence of fans or air conditioning, damp cloths
applied to the skin or cool baths can help regulate your body temperature.
Recognize the early signs of heat-related illnesses. Heat exhaustion symptoms
include heavy sweating, dizziness, nausea, headache, and rapid pulse.
If you or someone around you experiences these symptoms, immediately
move to a cool place, drink water, and apply cold compresses. I
n more severe cases, heatstroke can occur, characterized by confusion,
hot and dry skin, loss of consciousness, or high body temperature (above 103°F).
Heatstroke is a medical emergency—call for help immediately and try to cool
the person down while waiting for medical professionals.
